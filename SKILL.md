# Health Wellness Blog SEO

## Core Standard

Produce health and wellness content that is useful, readable, search-aware, and safety-aware. Treat health content as YMYL: prioritize clarity, humility, evidence quality, and reader safety over conversion pressure or ranking tricks.

Never present the article as medical advice, diagnosis, treatment, cure, or guaranteed prevention. Never tell readers to start, stop, or change medication, supplements, diet therapy, exercise therapy, or clinical care without a qualified professional.

## Workflow

1. Clarify the assignment when needed:
   - Topic, target reader, market or country, brand voice, format, length, funnel stage, primary keyword, secondary keywords, internal pages to link, products or services mentioned, and whether citations are required.
   - If missing details are not blocking, proceed with explicit assumptions.

2. Classify risk before drafting:
   - Low risk: general wellness habits, broad education, non-clinical lifestyle tips.
   - Medium risk: supplements, exercise plans, weight management, sleep, stress, nutrition for common conditions.
   - High risk: disease treatment, pregnancy, children, mental health crises, eating disorders, medication interactions, chronic conditions, acute symptoms, medical devices, regulated claims.
   - For medium or high risk, soften claims, add professional-care guidance, and require source verification before final publication.

3. Build the search-intent brief:
   - Primary intent: informational, commercial investigation, local, transactional, or mixed.
   - Reader job-to-be-done, likely concerns, beginner misconceptions, and safety-sensitive points.
   - Primary keyword, secondary terms, entities, related questions, and content gaps.
   - Suggested angle that satisfies search intent without exaggerating health benefits.
   - E-E-A-T plan: expert review, author credentials, citations, update cadence, and medical disclaimer placement.

4. Create the SEO package:
   - 5 to 10 SEO title options with realistic benefit language.
   - 2 to 4 meta descriptions under about 155 characters when possible.
   - Slug, H1, suggested H2/H3 outline, FAQ questions, schema ideas, image alt-text ideas, and internal-link opportunities.
   - Keep titles specific, useful, and non-alarmist. Avoid clickbait, fear framing, or unsupported superlatives.

5. Draft the article:
   - Start with the reader's immediate question and practical context.
   - Separate established evidence, emerging evidence, expert consensus, and practical tips.
   - Use plain language, short sections, scannable headings, and examples.
   - Include safety notes near the relevant recommendation, not only in a footer.
   - Use responsible CTAs such as "talk with a qualified clinician," "learn more," "download the checklist," or "book a coaching consultation" without implying guaranteed health outcomes.

6. Audit before delivery:
   - Run a claim audit, source plan, fact-check checklist, and publication-readiness review.
   - Flag claims that need citations or expert review instead of inventing sources.
   - Include a concise disclaimer when health advice could be mistaken for medical guidance.

## Output Patterns

For a brief, include:
- Search intent
- Audience and funnel stage
- Keyword cluster
- SERP/content angle
- Reader questions
- Safety risks
- Source plan
- Outline
- SEO title and meta options
- Internal-link ideas

For a draft, include:
- SEO title, meta description, slug, and H1
- Article body with evidence-aware wording
- FAQ section
- Practical tips
- Safety disclaimer
- Responsible CTA
- Claim audit table
- Publication checklist

For an audit, include:
- Risk rating
- Unsupported or overstrong claims
- Safer replacement wording
- Missing citations
- Medical review needs
- SEO gaps
- Final publish/no-publish recommendation

## Safety Language

Prefer cautious wording:
- "may help support"
- "is associated with"
- "research suggests"
- "some people find"
- "talk with a qualified professional if..."
- "not a substitute for medical care"

Avoid unsafe or overcertain wording unless directly supported by strong evidence and appropriate regulation:
- "cures"
- "treats"
- "prevents"
- "reverses"
- "detoxes"
- "clinically proven" without precise evidence
- "doctor recommended" without proof
- "safe for everyone"
- "no side effects"
- "guaranteed results"

## Evidence Rules

Use current, authoritative sources when the user asks for citations, when claims are clinical or product-adjacent, or when accuracy may have changed. Prioritize systematic reviews, clinical guidelines, government health agencies, academic medical centers, professional associations, peer-reviewed studies, and official product labels where relevant.

Do not fabricate citations, study details, statistics, expert quotes, regulatory approvals, or consensus. If sources are not checked, label the source plan as "to verify" and mark specific claims needing verification.
